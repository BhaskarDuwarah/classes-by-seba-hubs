Metals and Non-metals - Notes

üìñ Summary

This chapter explains the physical and chemical properties of metals and non-metals, their uses, and important reactions. Metals like iron, copper, and aluminium are malleable, ductile, and good conductors, while non-metals like sulphur and phosphorus are brittle and poor conductors. The reactivity series is introduced to predict displacement reactions. Metals form positive ions (cations) by losing electrons, while non-metals form negative ions (anions) by gaining electrons. Ionic bonding in compounds like NaCl and MgCl2 is discussed. Important chemical reactions include:

  • Reaction with oxygen: 4Na + O2 ‚Üí 2Na2O
  • Reaction with water: 2K + 2H2O ‚Üí 2KOH + H2
  • Reaction with acids: Zn + 2HCl ‚Üí ZnCl2 + H2‚Üë

The chapter also covers the extraction of metals (mining, concentration, reduction, refining) and corrosion of metals like rusting of iron: 4Fe + 3O2 + 6H2O ‚Üí 4Fe(OH)3. Prevention methods include galvanization, painting, and alloying. The uses of alloys like brass, bronze, and stainless steel are also highlighted.

‚úèÔ∏è 15 Short Answer Questions (2 Marks)

Q1 (2 Marks): Differentiate between metals and non-metals (two points).

A: (i) Metals are malleable and ductile; non-metals are brittle.
(ii) Metals are good conductors of heat and electricity; non-metals are poor conductors.

Q2 (2 Marks): Why is sodium stored in kerosene oil?

A: Sodium reacts vigorously with oxygen and water in air. Kerosene prevents its contact with air and moisture.

Q3 (2 Marks): Write two uses of aluminium.

A: (i) Making aircraft bodies due to its light weight.
(ii) Used in electrical transmission lines as it is a good conductor.

Q4 (2 Marks): Why does iron rust? Write the chemical reaction.

A: Iron rusts due to reaction with oxygen and moisture forming hydrated iron oxide: 4Fe + 3O2 + 6H2O ‚Üí 4Fe(OH)3

Q5 (2 Marks): Why are ionic compounds hard and have high melting points?

A: Due to strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions which require more energy to break.

Q6 (2 Marks): Name the metal: (i) Found in liquid state at room temperature. (ii) Stored under water.

A: (i) Mercury (Hg).
(ii) None; highly reactive metals like sodium are stored under kerosene, not water.

Q7 (2 Marks): Write the reaction of zinc with dilute sulphuric acid.

A: Zn + H2SO4 ‚Üí ZnSO4 + H2‚Üë

Q8 (2 Marks): Why are metals good conductors of electricity?

A: Due to free electrons in the metallic lattice which carry electric current.

Q9 (2 Marks): Why is copper used to make electrical wires?

A: Copper is a good conductor of electricity and is ductile, so it can be drawn into wires easily.

Q10 (2 Marks): Why are non-metals poor conductors of electricity?

A: Non-metals lack free electrons or ions needed to conduct electricity.

Q11 (2 Marks): Write the reaction of magnesium with water.

A: Mg + H2O ‚Üí Mg(OH)2 + H2‚Üë

Q12 (2 Marks): Define corrosion. Give an example.

A: Slow deterioration of metals due to chemical reactions with the environment. Example: Rusting of iron.

Q13 (2 Marks): What is an alloy? Write two examples.

A: Alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals or a metal and a non-metal. Examples: Brass (Cu + Zn), Bronze (Cu + Sn).

Q14 (2 Marks): Why does aluminium develop a white coating on exposure to air?

A: Aluminium reacts with oxygen to form a protective layer of aluminium oxide (Al2O3).

Q15 (2 Marks): Write the reactivity series for the following metals in decreasing order: Zn, Fe, Cu, Na.

A: Na > Zn > Fe > Cu.

Q1 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Differentiate between metals and non-metals (four points).

A: (i) Metals are malleable and ductile; non-metals are brittle.
(ii) Metals conduct heat and electricity; non-metals do not.
(iii) Metals form basic oxides; non-metals form acidic oxides.
(iv) Metals lose electrons to form cations; non-metals gain electrons to form anions.

Q2 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Explain the formation of ionic compounds with an example.

A: Ionic compounds form when metals transfer electrons to non-metals. Example: Na (2,8,1) loses 1 electron ‚Üí Na+; Cl (2,8,7) gains 1 electron ‚Üí Cl-. Forms NaCl by electrostatic attraction.

Q3 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Write two chemical properties of metals with reactions.

A: (i) Reaction with oxygen: 4Na + O2 ‚Üí 2Na2O
(ii) Reaction with acids: Zn + 2HCl ‚Üí ZnCl2 + H2‚Üë

Q4 (3‚Äì4 Marks): What is corrosion? How can it be prevented?

A: Corrosion is the slow destruction of metals by air and moisture. Prevention: (i) Painting (ii) Galvanization (iii) Alloying

Q5 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Explain the process of extraction of metals from their ores (general steps).

A: (i) Concentration of ore: Removal of impurities.
(ii) Reduction: Conversion of metal oxides to metal.
(iii) Refining: Purification of crude metal.

Q6 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Why is iron galvanized? How does galvanization protect it?

A: Galvanization coats iron with a layer of zinc. Zinc prevents air and moisture contact. Even if scratched, zinc corrodes first (sacrificial protection).

Q7 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Write the steps to show how zinc displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.

A: Zn + CuSO4 ‚Üí ZnSO4 + Cu
Observation: Blue colour of CuSO4 fades and reddish-brown copper is deposited.

Q8 (3‚Äì4 Marks): What are alloys? Write their advantages with examples.

A: Alloys are homogeneous mixtures of metals or metals with non-metals. Advantages: Stronger, resistant to corrosion. Examples: Brass (Cu + Zn), Bronze (Cu + Sn).

Q9 (3‚Äì4 Marks): What are amphoteric oxides? Give two examples with reactions.

A: Oxides that react with both acids and bases. Examples: Al2O3: Al2O3 + 6HCl ‚Üí 2AlCl3 + 3H2O
Al2O3 + 2NaOH ‚Üí 2NaAlO2 + H2O

Q10 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Write differences between ionic and covalent compounds (three points).

A: (i) Ionic: High melting point; Covalent: Low melting point.
(ii) Ionic: Conducts electricity in molten/solution state; Covalent: Poor conductor.
(iii) Ionic: Formed by transfer of electrons; Covalent: Formed by sharing of electrons.

üåü Important Previous Year Questions

Q1: Why is sodium kept immersed in kerosene?

A: Sodium reacts vigorously with moisture and oxygen in air, so it is kept in kerosene to prevent accidental fire.

Q2: Why is aluminium used for making aircraft bodies?

A: Aluminium is light, strong, malleable, and resistant to corrosion because of its protective oxide layer.

Q3: Write the chemical reaction when iron nails are dipped in copper sulphate solution.

A: Fe + CuSO4 ‚Üí FeSO4 + Cu
Observation: Blue colour of CuSO4 fades and reddish-brown copper is deposited on iron nails.

Q4: Why is galvanization necessary for iron articles?

A: To protect iron from rusting by coating it with a layer of zinc.

Q5: Explain why ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points.

A: Strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions require large amounts of energy to break.

Q6: What is an amphoteric oxide? Write equations showing its reaction with acid and base.

A: Amphoteric oxides react with both acids and bases.
Example: Al2O3
Al2O3 + 6HCl ‚Üí 2AlCl3 + 3H2O
Al2O3 + 2NaOH ‚Üí 2NaAlO2 + H2O

Q7: Why does copper not displace zinc from zinc sulphate solution?

A: Copper is less reactive than zinc; hence it cannot displace zinc from its compound.

Q8: Define alloy and give two examples with their constituents.

A: An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals or a metal with a non-metal.
Examples:
(i) Brass: Copper + Zinc
(ii) Bronze: Copper + Tin

Q9: Write one example each for: (a) A metal oxide which is acidic, (b) A metal oxide which is basic.

A: (a) Acidic: SiO2 (b) Basic: Na2O

Q10: Why is iron not used to make electric wires?

A: Iron is not ductile enough, and it rusts easily which affects conductivity and durability.
