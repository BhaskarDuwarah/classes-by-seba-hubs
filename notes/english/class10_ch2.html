Q1 (1 Mark): Define an acid with one example.

A: A substance that releases H+ ions in aqueous solution. Example: HCl.

Q2 (1 Mark): Define a base with one example.

A: A substance that releases OH- ions in aqueous solution. Example: NaOH.

Q3 (1 Mark): What is a neutralization reaction?

A: A reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water.

Q4 (1 Mark): Name the indicator that turns pink in basic solution.

A: Phenolphthalein.

Q5 (1 Mark): What is the pH of pure water?

A: pH = 7 (neutral).

Q6 (1 Mark): Name the gas evolved when an acid reacts with a metal carbonate.

A: Carbon dioxide (CO2).

Q7 (1 Mark): Write the chemical formula of baking soda.

A: NaHCO3.

Q8 (1 Mark): What is the chemical name of washing soda?

A: Sodium carbonate decahydrate (Na2CO3¬∑10H2O).

Q9 (1 Mark): Name the acid present in vinegar.

A: Acetic acid (CH3COOH).

A: It turns red.

‚úèÔ∏è 15 Short Answer Questions (2 Marks)

Q1 (2 Marks): Differentiate between acids and bases with one example each.

A: Acids: Substances that release H+ ions (Example: HCl). Bases: Substances that release OH- ions (Example: NaOH).

Q2 (2 Marks): What happens when zinc granules react with dilute HCl? Write the reaction.

A: Hydrogen gas is released with effervescence. Zn + 2HCl ‚Üí ZnCl2 + H2

Q3 (2 Marks): Why does an aqueous solution of an acid conduct electricity?

A: Acids ionize in water to produce H+ ions, which carry electric current.

Q4 (2 Marks): What is the importance of pH in everyday life? (Any two points)

A: (i) pH of soil affects crop growth. (ii) pH of stomach acid helps digestion.

Q5 (2 Marks): Write the reaction between NaOH and HCl. Name the type of reaction.

A: NaOH + HCl ‚Üí NaCl + H2O Type: Neutralization reaction.

Q6 (2 Marks): Why is Plaster of Paris stored in airtight containers?

A: To prevent it from reacting with moisture and converting into gypsum (CaSO4¬∑2H2O).

Q7 (2 Marks): Name the acid and base used to prepare sodium chloride in the lab.

A: Hydrochloric acid (HCl) and Sodium hydroxide (NaOH).

Q8 (2 Marks): What is the difference between strong and weak acids? Give examples.

A: Strong acids completely ionize in water (HCl). Weak acids partially ionize (CH3COOH).

Q9 (2 Marks): What is baking soda? Write its formula and one use.

A: Baking soda is sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO3). Use: Used in baking to make cakes fluffy.

Q10 (2 Marks): Write the chemical reaction to prepare washing soda from baking soda.

A: 2NaHCO3 ‚Üí Na2CO3 + CO2 + H2O (on heating)

Q11 (2 Marks): What happens when CO2 is passed through lime water? Write the reaction.

A: Lime water turns milky. Ca(OH)2 + CO2 ‚Üí CaCO3 + H2O

Q12 (2 Marks): Why is it important to control the pH of soil?

A: Different plants require specific pH for proper growth. For acidic soil, bases like lime are added to neutralize it.

Q13 (2 Marks): What is a neutral solution? Give an example.

A: A solution with pH 7 is neutral. Example: Pure water.

Q14 (2 Marks): Name the acid present in: (i) Lemon juice (ii) Ant sting

A: (i) Citric acid (ii) Methanoic acid (formic acid)

Q15 (2 Marks): What are the constituents of bleaching powder? Write its use.

A: Constituents: Ca(OCl)2. Use: Disinfecting drinking water.

Q1 (3‚Äì4 Marks): What is the pH scale? Write its importance in everyday life.

A: The pH scale measures the acidity or basicity of a solution on a scale of 0 to 14. pH < 7: acidic, pH = 7: neutral, pH > 7: basic. Importance: (i) Soil pH affects crop yield. (ii) Acid rain (low pH) harms buildings and aquatic life. (iii) pH in the stomach helps digestion.

Q2 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Explain with equations: (a) Action of dilute HCl on zinc (b) Action of dilute HCl on sodium carbonate

A: (a) Zn + 2HCl ‚Üí ZnCl2 + H2‚Üë (b) Na2CO3 + 2HCl ‚Üí 2NaCl + H2O + CO2‚Üë

Q3 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Why is Plaster of Paris (POP) called so? How is it prepared?

A: It is called Plaster of Paris because it was first made in Paris. Preparation: Heating gypsum at 373K gives POP. CaSO4¬∑2H2O ‚Üí CaSO4¬∑¬ΩH2O + 1¬ΩH2O

Q4 (3‚Äì4 Marks): What are strong acids and weak acids? Give two examples each.

A: Strong acids: Completely ionize in water (HCl, H2SO4). Weak acids: Partially ionize in water (CH3COOH, H2CO3).

Q5 (3‚Äì4 Marks): What is bleaching powder? How is it prepared and used?

A: Bleaching powder is Ca(OCl)2. Prepared by: Ca(OH)2 + Cl2 ‚Üí Ca(OCl)2 + H2O. Uses: Disinfecting water, bleaching fabrics, deodorizing.

Q6 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Differentiate between strong and weak bases with examples.

A: Strong bases: Fully ionize in water (NaOH, KOH). Weak bases: Partially ionize in water (NH4OH, Mg(OH)2).

Q7 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Why does tooth decay start when pH of the mouth is below 5.5? How can it be prevented?

A: Acidic pH (below 5.5) dissolves enamel, leading to cavities. Prevention: Brushing teeth, using alkaline toothpaste, and avoiding acidic foods.

Q8 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Explain how washing soda is prepared from sodium carbonate and write its two uses.

A: Washing soda (Na2CO3¬∑10H2O) is prepared by recrystallizing sodium carbonate. Uses: (i) Softening hard water. (ii) Cleaning agents.

Q9 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Why does dry HCl gas not turn blue litmus red while aqueous HCl does?

A: Dry HCl does not ionize to produce H+ ions, but in water it forms H3O+ which turns litmus red.

Q10 (3‚Äì4 Marks): Explain neutralization reaction with an example from daily life.

A: In neutralization, an acid reacts with a base to form salt and water. Example: Antacid tablets neutralize excess stomach acid (HCl) using weak bases like Mg(OH)2.

üåü Important Questions (Previous Years)

Q1: Give the chemical name and formula of marble. Explain how marble is damaged by acid rain with a reaction.

A: Marble is calcium carbonate (CaCO3). Acid rain (H2SO4/HNO3) reacts with it: CaCO3 + H2SO4 ‚Üí CaSO4 + CO2 + H2O. This causes surface erosion and damage to structures like the Taj Mahal.

Q2: Why should concentrated acids be diluted by adding acid into water and not water into acid?

A: Adding water to acid releases large heat suddenly causing splashes. Adding acid slowly to water allows heat to dissipate safely.

Q3: Explain why dry HCl gas does not turn blue litmus red but aqueous HCl does.

A: Dry HCl does not ionize to release H+ ions. In water, HCl forms H3O+, which turns litmus red.

Q4: Why is HCl a strong acid and CH3COOH a weak acid? Support with reason.

A: HCl fully ionizes in water, releasing H+. CH3COOH partially ionizes.

Q5: Explain the role of pH in soil and its importance for crops.

A: Crops require soil of specific pH for nutrient absorption. Acidic soil is neutralized using lime (CaO/Ca(OH)2).

Q6: What happens when: (a) Marble reacts with dilute HCl (b) CO2 is passed through lime water (initially and excess)

A: (a) CaCO3 + 2HCl ‚Üí CaCl2 + CO2 + H2O (b) Lime water turns milky: Ca(OH)2 + CO2 ‚Üí CaCO3 + H2O; Excess CO2 clears milkiness.

Q7: How do antacids work? Give an example.

A: Antacids neutralize excess stomach acid. Example: Mg(OH)2 + 2HCl ‚Üí MgCl2 + 2H2O.

Q8: Explain any two uses of baking soda with reactions.

A: (i) Baking: NaHCO3 + Heat ‚Üí Na2CO3 + CO2 + H2O (fluffiness) (ii) Fire extinguishers: NaHCO3 releases CO2 to smother flames.

Q9: Write the preparation and two uses of bleaching powder.

A: Preparation: Ca(OH)2 + Cl2 ‚Üí Ca(OCl)2 + H2O Uses: Disinfecting water, bleaching fabrics.

Q10: Classify these salts by pH as acidic, basic, or neutral: K2SO4, NH4Cl, Na2CO3, NaCl.

A: K2SO4: Neutral, NH4Cl: Acidic, Na2CO3: Basic, NaCl: Neutral.
